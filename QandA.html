LIS590-RO, Representing and Organizing Information Resources.

Q0: When does LIS590-RO meet this Fall? On campus or LEEP?

A0: LIS590-RO will meet Thursdays, 1:00-3:50pm. It is an on campus section this time.

Q1: What is LIS590-RO?

A1: LIS590-RO is a class on resource analysis and description. You'll be taught how to analyze any domain or genre of natural or cultural resources, and develop a framework for precise descriptions in that genre or domain.

Q2: What is a resource in the context of this class? What is a domain or genre?

A2: A resource can be any identifiable thing: a physical object like a shoe or a pencil, an abstract object like a proposition or an algorithm, or a social object like a mortgage or a marriage. Domains and genres are groups or categories of resource that we identify: silver Mercury dimes, for example, or Vietnam War era diplomatic communications, or coral reefs, or local optometrists, or large prime numbers.

Q3: This sounds like things we do in other GSLIS classes, like cataloging, database design, and metadata. How is RO different, and why wouldn't I enroll one of those other classes instead?

A3: Other GSLIS courses in the IO/KR area focus on specific resource genres, specialized description principles, and only a limited number of standard notations. They're important, but LIS590-RO gives you a broader view on the same problems. In a curriculum without many course sequences, RO serves as a reinforcing complement to your other classes. More importantly for your career, RO starts you practicing skills that will help you adapt to changing technology, reforms to professional practice, and new types of collections that we have no way of predicting. Enroll in LIS590-RO with the next ten years of your working life in mind.

Q4: What do students actually do in LIS590-RO?

A4: You choose a domain or genre of resources that interests you, and complete a series of linked analysis exercises that examine the genre in depth. Class participation involves discussions where you help your classmates answer the same difficult questions about an entirely different domain than the one you're examining. If you enroll for four credits you also write a term project on a common problem in analysis and description (like identity, provenance, or expressive power).

Q5: This is sounding theoretical. Is RO a theoretical or an applied class?

A5: Some of the ideas and concepts are abstract, drawing on work in philosophy and linguistics. But readings are typically taken from textbooks or treatments written for an educated general audience—they're not academic research papers. The exercises are highly applied: it's like you're inventing a metadata standard from the ground up.

Q6: Does LIS590-RO satisfy requirements for any of the degree specializations?

A6: LIS590-RO contributes to fulfilling a specific requirement for the Data Curation specialization. The class provides very helpful preparation for core classes and electives in the Digital Libraries CAS and the Socio-technical Data Analytics specialization. But LIS590-RO is mainly a generalization class, not a specialization class.
